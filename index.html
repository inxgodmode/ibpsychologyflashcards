HM: Milner (1966)

What was HM's background and what led to his surgery?
HM was born in 1926 in Manchester, Connecticut. At age 7, he was hit by a cyclist, causing a head injury. Epileptic seizures started at age 10, likely due to the accident. By age 27, seizures were so severe that he couldn’t lead a normal life. Neurosurgeon William Scoville performed an experimental surgery, removing tissue from the medial temporal lobe, including the hippocampus, on both sides of HM’s brain.
What was the aim of Milner’s (1966) study on HM?
To investigate the effects of HM’s surgery (removal of the hippocampus and medial temporal lobe) on his memory and cognitive functions, particularly the role of the hippocampus in memory formation.
What research methods did Milner use to study HM?
Milner used method triangulation: psychometric testing (IQ tests), direct observation of behavior, interviews with HM and his family, cognitive testing (memory recall and learning tasks like reverse mirror drawing), and later MRI scans (by Corkin in 1992 and 2003) to assess brain damage.
What were the key findings about HM’s memory after the surgery?
HM had severe anterograde amnesia (couldn’t form new episodic or semantic memories) and mild retrograde amnesia (lost about 1 year of memories before the surgery). He retained childhood memories, procedural memories (e.g., mowing a lawn), and working memory (e.g., recalling numbers with rehearsal). He could form cognitive maps (e.g., drawing his house layout) but couldn’t recall learning new skills like reverse mirror drawing.
How does HM’s case study demonstrate localization of function in the brain?
HM’s case shows the hippocampus is critical for transferring short-term memories to long-term memory. Its removal caused anterograde amnesia, but short-term and procedural memories were intact, indicating these are stored elsewhere in the brain.
What brain study technique was used in HM’s case, and what did it reveal?
MRI scans (conducted by Corkin in 1992 and 2003) revealed damage to HM’s hippocampus and medial temporal lobe. This confirmed the hippocampus’s role in memory formation, as its damage explained HM’s inability to form new long-term memories.
How does HM’s case study demonstrate research methods in the cognitive approach?
It’s a longitudinal case study using method triangulation (psychometric testing, interviews, observation, cognitive tasks). This allowed in-depth analysis of HM’s memory deficits over 50 years, providing rich qualitative and quantitative data.
How does HM’s case evaluate the Multi-Store Model of memory?
HM’s case supports the Multi-Store Model by showing a distinction between short-term memory (intact, as he could hold conversations) and long-term memory (impaired, as he couldn’t form new memories). However, it challenges the model by showing procedural memory and cognitive maps were intact, suggesting long-term memory has multiple stores not accounted for in the model.
What are the strengths of Milner’s study on HM?
Longitudinal design (over 50 years) allowed observation of changes over time. Method triangulation provided comprehensive data. High ecological validity, as HM was studied in his natural environment. Ethical standards were met (consent, confidentiality, protection from harm).
What are the limitations of Milner’s study on HM?
As a case study, it’s hard to replicate, limiting generalizability. Some data was retrospective, lacking pre-accident cognitive baselines. Epilepsy medication may have contributed to brain damage, though this is less relevant since the focus is on the hippocampus’s role.

Maguire (2000)

What was the aim of Maguire’s (2000) study?
To investigate whether the brains of London taxi drivers differ due to their extensive navigation experience and knowledge of the city, specifically looking for changes in the hippocampus.
Who were the participants in Maguire’s (2000) study?
16 right-handed male London taxi drivers who had completed the "Knowledge" test and held a license for at least 1.5 years, compared with MRI scans of 50 right-handed male non-taxi drivers from an MRI database, matched for age.
What methods did Maguire (2000) use to study the brain?
Used MRI scans analyzed with two techniques: voxel-based morphometry (VBM) to measure grey matter density, and pixel counting to calculate the hippocampal area. It was a single-blind, correlational study examining years of taxi driving and brain anatomy.
What were the key findings of Maguire’s (2000) study?
Taxi drivers had larger posterior hippocampi and smaller anterior hippocampi compared to controls. The right posterior hippocampus volume correlated with years spent as a taxi driver. No differences were found in other brain areas.
How does Maguire (2000) demonstrate techniques for studying the brain?
Used MRI scans with voxel-based morphometry (VBM) to measure grey matter density and pixel counting to assess hippocampal size, showing how MRI can map brain structure changes related to behavior (navigation).
How does Maguire (2000) relate to neuroplasticity?
Shows that the hippocampus can change in response to environmental demands (navigation experience). The correlation between years of taxi driving and posterior hippocampus size suggests the brain adapts structurally to spatial demands.
How does Maguire (2000) demonstrate localization of function?
Indicates the posterior hippocampus is involved in using previously learned spatial information (navigation in taxi drivers), while the anterior hippocampus may be more involved in encoding new spatial layouts.
What are the strengths of Maguire’s (2000) study?
Single-blind analysis reduced researcher bias. MRI has high ecological validity as participants weren’t tasked during scans. The correlation with years of driving supports neuroplasticity over innate differences. Ethically sound (consent, no health risks).
What are the limitations of Maguire’s (2000) study?
Quasi-experiment, so no cause-and-effect can be established. Sampling bias (all male participants) limits generalizability. Cannot fully rule out that those with larger hippocampi are drawn to taxi driving, though correlation with experience counters this.

Draganski (2004)

What is the background concept of neuroplasticity in Draganski (2004)?
Neuroplasticity is the brain’s ability to change its neural structures in response to the environment and cognition. It involves dendritic branching (via Long Term Potentiation, LTP) from repeated tasks, increasing neural density, and neural pruning when behaviors stop, reducing unused synaptic connections.
What was the aim of Draganski’s (2004) study?
To investigate whether learning a new skill (juggling) would affect the brain’s structure, specifically looking for changes in grey matter density as evidence of neuroplasticity.
Who were the participants in Draganski’s (2004) study?
24 volunteers aged 20–24 (21 females, 3 males), all non-jugglers at the start. They were divided into two groups: jugglers (taught to juggle) and non-jugglers (control group).
What was the procedure of Draganski’s (2004) study?
All participants had an initial MRI scan. The juggling group was taught a three-ball cascade routine, practiced until mastered, then had a second MRI scan. They stopped juggling for three months and had a third MRI scan. The non-juggling group had MRIs at the same intervals. Voxel-based morphometry (VBM) analyzed grey matter density.
What were the key findings of Draganski’s (2004) study?
After mastering juggling, the juggling group showed increased grey matter in the mid-temporal area (visual memory) of both hemispheres. Three months after stopping, grey matter decreased in these areas. The non-juggling group showed no changes throughout the study.
How does Draganski (2004) demonstrate neuroplasticity?
Shows that learning a skill (juggling) increases grey matter in the mid-temporal area (via LTP and dendritic branching), and stopping the skill leads to neural pruning, reducing grey matter, demonstrating the brain’s structural adaptability.
How does Draganski (2004) relate to the formation of synapses and neural pruning?
Juggling increased neural density via synapse formation (LTP and dendritic branching) in the mid-temporal area. Stopping juggling led to neural pruning, reducing grey matter as unused synaptic connections were eliminated.
What research methods does Draganski (2004) use to study the brain?
Experimental pre-test, post-test design with a control group. Used MRI scans and voxel-based morphometry (VBM) to measure changes in grey matter density over time, showing structural brain changes due to behavior.
What are the strengths of Draganski’s (2004) study?
Experimental design with a control group supports cause-and-effect (juggling caused brain changes). Pre-test, post-test design tracks changes over time. VBM provides precise measurement of grey matter changes.
What are the limitations of Draganski’s (2004) study?
Small sample size (24 participants) may reduce reliability of averages. Internal validity issues as participants practiced at home. Needs replication to confirm reliability. Gender imbalance (mostly female) may limit generalizability.

Loftus & Palmer (1974)

What concept did Loftus & Palmer (1974) investigate, and how does it relate to Bartlett (1932)?
Investigated reconstructive memory—how schemas influence memory. Bartlett (1932) showed cultural schemas distort memory by fitting experiences into existing knowledge. Loftus & Palmer tested how leading questions (post-event information) distort memory, like speed estimates in car accidents.
What was the aim of Experiment 1 in Loftus & Palmer (1974)?
To investigate whether leading questions (using different verbs) affect the estimation of speed in a car accident viewed on film, testing the influence of wording on memory.
What was the procedure of Experiment 1 in Loftus & Palmer (1974)?
45 students were divided into 5 groups (independent samples design). They watched 7 films of car accidents (5–30 seconds long). After each film, they gave an account of the accident and answered a questionnaire. The critical question varied: “About how fast were the cars going when they [smashed/collided/bumped/hit/contacted] each other?” Speed estimates were recorded.
What were the results of Experiment 1 in Loftus & Palmer (1974)?
Mean speed estimates varied by verb: “smashed” (40.8 mph), “collided” (39.3 mph), “bumped” (38.1 mph), “hit” (34.0 mph), “contacted” (31.8 mph). Results were significant (p ≤ 0.005), showing the verb influenced speed estimates, likely due to schema activation or response bias.
What was the aim of Experiment 2 in Loftus & Palmer (1974)?
To investigate if leading questions causing higher speed estimates (e.g., “smashed”) would lead participants to falsely recall broken glass in the accident, testing schema processing in reconstructive memory.
What was the procedure of Experiment 2 in Loftus & Palmer (1974)?
150 students were randomly assigned to 3 groups. They watched a 1-minute film of a car accident (4 seconds of crash). Group 1 was asked, “How fast were the cars going when they smashed into each other?” Group 2 was asked the same with “hit.” Group 3 wasn’t asked about speed. One week later, all answered a questionnaire with the critical question: “Did you see any broken glass?” (There was none in the film.)
What were the results of Experiment 2 in Loftus & Palmer (1974)?
“Smashed” group estimated higher speeds (10.46 mph) than “hit” group (8.00 mph). More in the “smashed” group (16) reported seeing broken glass compared to “hit” (7) and control (6). Chi-square test was significant (p ≤ 0.025), showing “smashed” led to more false memories of broken glass due to schema activation.
How does Loftus & Palmer (1974) demonstrate the reliability of cognitive processes?
Shows memory (reconstructive memory) is unreliable. Leading questions (e.g., “smashed” vs. “hit”) distort recall, as schemas activated by wording change memory (e.g., higher speed estimates, false recall of broken glass), highlighting memory’s susceptibility to post-event information.
What research methods does Loftus & Palmer (1974) use in the cognitive approach?
Lab experiments with controlled variables (IV: verb in question; DV: speed estimate, recall of broken glass). Independent samples design (Experiment 1) and random allocation (Experiment 2). Used statistical tests (p ≤ 0.005, p ≤ 0.025) to confirm significance, establishing cause-and-effect between leading questions and memory distortion.
What are the strengths of Loftus & Palmer’s (1974) study?
Controlled lab experiment minimized confounding variables, establishing cause-and-effect (leading questions distort memory). Statistical significance (p ≤ 0.005, p ≤ 0.025) supports reliability of findings. Practical application to eyewitness testimony in court settings.
What are the limitations of Loftus & Palmer’s (1974) study?
Low ecological validity—watching films lacks the emotional impact of real accidents, limiting generalizability. Student sample (young, inexperienced drivers) may not represent the general population. Artificial setting (lab) may not reflect real-life memory processes.

Newcomer et al (1999)

What was the aim of Newcomer et al (1999)?
To investigate whether high levels of the stress hormone cortisol interfere with verbal declarative memory.
Who were the participants in Newcomer et al (1999)?
Employees or students at Washington University Medical Center, screened to exclude those who were pregnant, had a history of mental illness, head trauma, or corticosteroid-treated illnesses. Participants were matched for age and gender.
What was the procedure of Newcomer et al (1999)?
A double-blind lab experiment with three conditions: high cortisol (160 mg/day, simulating major stress), low cortisol (40 mg/day, simulating minor stress), and placebo. Over 4 days, participants listened to and recalled a prose paragraph daily, with tests before, 1 day after, 4 days after, and 6 days after to check for long-term effects.
What were the key results of Newcomer et al (1999)?
High cortisol group showed the worst performance in verbal declarative memory, with impaired recall of prose. No difference between low dose and placebo groups. Effects were temporary; memory returned to normal after stopping cortisol. Low dose and placebo groups improved over time due to practice effects.
How does Newcomer et al (1999) relate to the function of hormones in human behavior?
Demonstrates that cortisol, a stress hormone, impairs verbal declarative memory (a behavior) at high levels, showing how hormones influence cognitive behavior under stress.
What research methods does Newcomer et al (1999) use in the biological approach?
Double-blind lab experiment with a control group (placebo). Used baseline testing to control for individual differences and counterbalanced prose texts to control for difficulty, establishing a causal link between cortisol and memory performance.
What are the strengths of Newcomer et al (1999)?
Experimental design established cause-and-effect (cortisol impaired memory). Baseline testing controlled for individual differences. Counterbalancing prose texts controlled for difficulty. Double-blind method reduced bias.
What are the limitations of Newcomer et al (1999)?
Limited control over extraneous variables (e.g., participants’ stressors) as they weren’t in the lab the whole time. Memorizing prose lacks ecological validity, limiting generalizability. Ethical concerns as cortisol temporarily impaired memory, though participants gave informed consent and effects were not permanent.

Wedekind et al (1995)

What was the background concept of Wedekind et al (1995)?
Evolutionary psychologists argue behaviors result from natural selection. The Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) genes, crucial for the immune system, are co-dominantly expressed. Diverse MHC genes in parents lead to stronger offspring immunity, so humans may have evolved to detect MHC differences via smell (pheromones) for mate selection.
What was the aim of Wedekind et al (1995)?
To determine whether one's MHC affects mate choice, specifically if women prefer the scent of men with dissimilar MHC genes, supporting evolutionary mate selection.
Who were the participants in Wedekind et al (1995)?
49 female and 44 male students from the University of Bern, Switzerland. Women were from biology and psychology courses, men from chemistry, physics, and geography. Participants were MHC-typed for diversity, and it was noted if women were on oral contraceptives.
What was the procedure of Wedekind et al (1995)?
Men wore T-shirts for 2 nights, keeping them in open plastic bags during the day, using perfume-free detergent and soap, avoiding deodorants, alcohol, smoking, spicy foods, and sexual activity. Women, tested in the second week post-menstruation, ranked 7 T-shirts (3 MHC-similar, 3 MHC-dissimilar, 1 unworn control) for intensity, pleasantness, and sexiness. Women used nose spray for 14 days prior and read *Perfume* by Süskind to enhance smell sensitivity.
What were the key results of Wedekind et al (1995)?
Women rated MHC-dissimilar men’s T-shirts as more pleasant and sexier than MHC-similar ones. This preference reversed in women on oral contraceptives, suggesting MHC influences mate choice via pheromones, supporting evolutionary theory.
How does Wedekind et al (1995) relate to evolutionary explanations of behavior?
Supports evolutionary theory by showing women prefer mates with dissimilar MHC genes, likely to enhance offspring immunity, a trait selected for through natural selection in mate choice behavior.
How does Wedekind et al (1995) demonstrate the role of pheromones?
Shows pheromones (body odor linked to MHC) influence mate selection. Women’s preference for MHC-dissimilar scents suggests pheromones signal genetic compatibility, guiding reproductive behavior.
What research methods does Wedekind et al (1995) use in the biological approach?
Double-blind experiment minimized bias (neither participants nor researchers knew T-shirt MHC types). Controlled variables (e.g., no deodorants, specific timing for women) ensured focus on MHC effects on mate choice behavior.
What are the strengths of Wedekind et al (1995)?
Double-blind design reduced demand characteristics. Replicated by Jacob et al (2002) and supported by animal studies (e.g., Yamazaki et al, 1976). Ethical (consent, debriefing). Supports evolutionary theory with real-world implications (e.g., miscarriage rates linked to MHC similarity).
What are the limitations of Wedekind et al (1995)?
Sample (students, same age/culture) limits generalizability. Reductionist—focuses on MHC, ignoring cognitive/socio-cultural factors in mate choice. Oral contraceptive effects suggest hormonal influences may confound natural preferences.

Weissman et al (2005)

What are "kinship studies" as described in Weissman et al (2005)?
"Kinship studies" (termed family or pedigree studies) involve researching the incidence of a behavior, like Major Depressive Disorder, across generations (usually three) to explore potential genetic links.
What was the aim of Weissman et al (2005)?
To study the potential genetic nature of Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) by examining its incidence across three generations in families at high and low risk for depression.
Who were the participants in Weissman et al (2005)?
161 grandchildren, their parents, and grandparents. Grandparents were originally selected from an outpatient clinic for mood disorders (depressed) or the same community (non-depressed). Parents and children were interviewed four times over 20 years.
What was the procedure of Weissman et al (2005)?
A longitudinal family study over 20 years. Clinicians, blind to past diagnoses, interviewed participants four times. Children were evaluated by two clinicians (a child psychiatrist and a psychologist) for inter-rater reliability (0.82 for MDD, 0.65 for anxiety, 0.94 for alcohol dependency). Researcher triangulation ensured credibility.
What were the key results of Weissman et al (2005)?
Grandchildren with two generations of depression had high rates of psychiatric disorders (59.2% by age 12, often anxiety). Risk increased if both grandparents and parents had depression, and parental depression severity correlated with child mood disorders. No significant effect on grandchildren if only parents were depressed without grandparental history.
How does Weissman et al (2005) relate to "kinship studies" in the study of behavior?
Demonstrates how family studies ("kinship studies") identify genetic links to behavior by showing Major Depressive Disorder’s transmission across three generations, with higher rates in families with a history of depression in both parents and grandparents.
What are the strengths of Weissman et al (2005)?
Longitudinal design increases data reliability. Researcher triangulation enhances credibility. Large sample (161 children) for a family study. High inter-rater reliability (e.g., 0.82 for MDD) ensures diagnostic consistency.
What are the limitations of Weissman et al (2005)?
Time spent with healthy grandparents may be a confounding variable. No genotype was studied, so genetic links are inferred, not confirmed. More research needed to confirm reliability despite the large sample.

Rosenzweig, Bennet & Diamond (1972)

What was the aim of Rosenzweig, Bennet & Diamond (1972)?
To investigate whether environmental factors (rich vs. impoverished environments) affect the development of neurons in the cerebral cortex, testing neuroplasticity in rats.
What was the procedure of Rosenzweig, Bennet & Diamond (1972)?
Three male rats from a common litter were randomly allocated to three conditions for 30–60 days: enriched condition (EC, 10–12 rats with toys/mazes), control condition (CC, 3 rats in a cage), and impoverished condition (IC, 1 rat in a cage without toys). All had food/water access. Rats were then killed to study brain anatomy changes.
What were the key results of Rosenzweig, Bennet & Diamond (1972)?
EC rats had increased thickness and weight of the cerebral cortex compared to IC rats. EC rats also showed greater activity of acetylcholine neurons, linked to learning and memory. A follow-up showed 30 minutes/day in an EC produced similar brain changes.
How does Rosenzweig, Bennet & Diamond (1972) demonstrate neuroplasticity?
Shows the brain changes due to experience—enriched environments increased cortex thickness and acetylcholine activity, while impoverished ones did not, proving the brain’s plasticity beyond childhood.
How and why are research methods used in Rosenzweig, Bennet & Diamond (1972) in the biological approach?
Used a controlled lab experiment with random allocation to conditions, isolating environmental variables (enrichment levels). This allowed a cause-and-effect relationship to be established between environment and brain changes, a key method in biological research for studying neuroplasticity.
How does Rosenzweig, Bennet & Diamond (1972) use animal models to understand human behavior and physiology?
Used rats to study neuroplasticity, assuming brain plasticity patterns are similar in rats and humans. Findings suggest human brains are also affected by environmental stimulation, relevant to cognitive development (e.g., poverty’s impact on children).
What are the ethical considerations in Rosenzweig, Bennet & Diamond (1972) for animal research?
Ethical concerns include undue stress (IC rats isolated in impoverished environments) and harm (rats were killed). A cost-benefit analysis must justify the study’s goals (understanding neuroplasticity) against the harm to animals.
What are the strengths of Rosenzweig, Bennet & Diamond (1972)?
Controlled lab experiment established cause-and-effect. Results replicated many times, increasing reliability. Findings have human implications (e.g., environmental stimulation’s role in cognitive development). A follow-up confirmed shorter EC exposure still produced brain changes.
What are the limitations of Rosenzweig, Bennet & Diamond (1972)?
Unclear if EC effects were due to toys, social interaction, or exercise—single rats in EC with toys showed no changes unless stimulated. Animal study, so generalizability to humans requires further research. Confounding variables (e.g., exercise vs. stimulation) not fully isolated.

Bartlett (1932)

What was the aim of Bartlett (1932)?
To investigate how memory of a story is affected by prior knowledge, testing if cultural background and unfamiliarity with a text lead to memory distortion through cultural schemas.
What was the procedure of Bartlett (1932)?
British participants were told a Native American story, *The War of the Ghosts*, which was unfamiliar to them. They were split into two conditions: repeated reproduction (recall the story multiple times over days/weeks/months/years) or serial reproduction (recall and retell the story to another person).
What were the key results of Bartlett (1932)?
No significant difference between the two conditions. Participants distorted the story through: assimilation (details changed to fit British cultural norms), leveling (story shortened, omitting unfamiliar details), and sharpening (order changed, familiar terms/emotions added). Main themes were recalled, but unfamiliar elements were altered to fit cultural expectations.
How does Bartlett (1932) evaluate schema theory?
Supports schema theory by showing memory is reconstructive—participants used cultural schemas to interpret and recall the story, altering unfamiliar elements to fit their British cultural expectations, demonstrating schemas’ role in memory processing.
To what extent does Bartlett (1932) show the reliability of one cognitive process?
Shows memory is unreliable due to reconstructive nature. Participants distorted *The War of the Ghosts* to fit cultural schemas, changing details, order, and adding emotions, indicating memory is an active process prone to bias rather than a faithful record.
How does Bartlett (1932) show sociocultural factors affecting cognitive processes?
Demonstrates that cultural background (sociocultural factor) affects memory (cognitive process). British participants altered the Native American story to align with their cultural schemas, showing culture influences how memory is reconstructed.
What are the strengths of Bartlett (1932)?
High ecological validity—explains real-life memory distortions (e.g., cultural influences on recall). Introduced reconstructive memory theory, widely applicable. Highlights active nature of memory and role of schemas in meaning-making.
What are the limitations of Bartlett (1932)?
Lacked rigorous control—non-standardized instructions and recall timing. No control group (e.g., Native Americans recalling the story) to confirm cultural distortion. Quasi-experimental (culture as IV), so no cause-and-effect established. IV (repeated vs. serial reproduction) had no effect on DV.

Tversky and Kahneman (1974)

What is the background concept of Tversky and Kahneman (1974)?
Anchoring bias, a cognitive bias, is the tendency to rely heavily on the first piece of information (the "anchor") when making decisions. It occurs due to heuristics in System 1 thinking (quick, intuitive), often because of cognitive load, ego depletion, or being cognitive misers, leading to systematic errors in judgment.
What was the aim of Tversky and Kahneman (1974)?
To investigate the effect of anchoring bias on numerical estimation, specifically whether the starting number in a sequence (ascending or descending) influences the estimated value of a multiplication problem.
What was the procedure of Tversky and Kahneman (1974)?
High school students were split into two groups. The ascending condition group estimated 1 × 2 × 3 × 4 × 5 × 6 × 7 × 8 in 5 seconds. The descending condition group estimated 8 × 7 × 6 × 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 in 5 seconds. The hypothesis was that the first number (1 or 8) would anchor their estimates.
What were the key results of Tversky and Kahneman (1974)?
The median estimate for the ascending group (starting with 1) was 512, while the descending group (starting with 8) was 2250. The actual value is 40320, showing both groups underestimated, but the descending group’s estimate was significantly higher due to the anchor effect.
How does Tversky and Kahneman (1974) show the reliability of cognitive processes?
Demonstrates decision-making (a cognitive process) is unreliable due to anchoring bias. Participants’ estimates were heavily influenced by the first number (1 or 8), showing heuristics lead to systematic errors in judgment rather than accurate reasoning.
What are the strengths of Tversky and Kahneman (1974)?
Simple, replicable experiment with high internal validity—controlled conditions allowed a causal link between anchor and estimate. Use of median reduced outlier influence. Supported by other studies (e.g., Plassman et al, 2008) showing anchoring in real-world contexts.
What are the limitations of Tversky and Kahneman (1974)?
Low ecological validity—estimating a math problem in 5 seconds is artificial, limiting real-world applicability. Independent samples design may introduce participant variability (e.g., math skills). A matched pairs design would better control for this.
