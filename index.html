HM: Milner (1966)

What was HM's background and what led to his surgery?
HM was born in 1926 in Manchester, Connecticut. At age 7, he was hit by a cyclist, causing a head injury. Epileptic seizures started at age 10, likely due to the accident. By age 27, seizures were so severe that he couldn’t lead a normal life. Neurosurgeon William Scoville performed an experimental surgery, removing tissue from the medial temporal lobe, including the hippocampus, on both sides of HM’s brain.
What was the aim of Milner’s (1966) study on HM?
To investigate the effects of HM’s surgery (removal of the hippocampus and medial temporal lobe) on his memory and cognitive functions, particularly the role of the hippocampus in memory formation.
What research methods did Milner use to study HM?
Milner used method triangulation: psychometric testing (IQ tests), direct observation of behavior, interviews with HM and his family, cognitive testing (memory recall and learning tasks like reverse mirror drawing), and later MRI scans (by Corkin in 1992 and 2003) to assess brain damage.
What were the key findings about HM’s memory after the surgery?
HM had severe anterograde amnesia (couldn’t form new episodic or semantic memories) and mild retrograde amnesia (lost about 1 year of memories before the surgery). He retained childhood memories, procedural memories (e.g., mowing a lawn), and working memory (e.g., recalling numbers with rehearsal). He could form cognitive maps (e.g., drawing his house layout) but couldn’t recall learning new skills like reverse mirror drawing.
How does HM’s case study demonstrate localization of function in the brain?
HM’s case shows the hippocampus is critical for transferring short-term memories to long-term memory. Its removal caused anterograde amnesia, but short-term and procedural memories were intact, indicating these are stored elsewhere in the brain.
What brain study technique was used in HM’s case, and what did it reveal?
MRI scans (conducted by Corkin in 1992 and 2003) revealed damage to HM’s hippocampus and medial temporal lobe. This confirmed the hippocampus’s role in memory formation, as its damage explained HM’s inability to form new long-term memories.
How does HM’s case study demonstrate research methods in the cognitive approach?
It’s a longitudinal case study using method triangulation (psychometric testing, interviews, observation, cognitive tasks). This allowed in-depth analysis of HM’s memory deficits over 50 years, providing rich qualitative and quantitative data.
How does HM’s case evaluate the Multi-Store Model of memory?
HM’s case supports the Multi-Store Model by showing a distinction between short-term memory (intact, as he could hold conversations) and long-term memory (impaired, as he couldn’t form new memories). However, it challenges the model by showing procedural memory and cognitive maps were intact, suggesting long-term memory has multiple stores not accounted for in the model.
What are the strengths of Milner’s study on HM?
Longitudinal design (over 50 years) allowed observation of changes over time. Method triangulation provided comprehensive data. High ecological validity, as HM was studied in his natural environment. Ethical standards were met (consent, confidentiality, protection from harm).
What are the limitations of Milner’s study on HM?
As a case study, it’s hard to replicate, limiting generalizability. Some data was retrospective, lacking pre-accident cognitive baselines. Epilepsy medication may have contributed to brain damage, though this is less relevant since the focus is on the hippocampus’s role.

Maguire (2000)

What was the aim of Maguire’s (2000) study?
To investigate whether the brains of London taxi drivers differ due to their extensive navigation experience and knowledge of the city, specifically looking for changes in the hippocampus.
Who were the participants in Maguire’s (2000) study?
16 right-handed male London taxi drivers who had completed the "Knowledge" test and held a license for at least 1.5 years, compared with MRI scans of 50 right-handed male non-taxi drivers from an MRI database, matched for age.
What methods did Maguire (2000) use to study the brain?
Used MRI scans analyzed with two techniques: voxel-based morphometry (VBM) to measure grey matter density, and pixel counting to calculate the hippocampal area. It was a single-blind, correlational study examining years of taxi driving and brain anatomy.
What were the key findings of Maguire’s (2000) study?
Taxi drivers had larger posterior hippocampi and smaller anterior hippocampi compared to controls. The right posterior hippocampus volume correlated with years spent as a taxi driver. No differences were found in other brain areas.
How does Maguire (2000) demonstrate techniques for studying the brain?
Used MRI scans with voxel-based morphometry (VBM) to measure grey matter density and pixel counting to assess hippocampal size, showing how MRI can map brain structure changes related to behavior (navigation).
How does Maguire (2000) relate to neuroplasticity?
Shows that the hippocampus can change in response to environmental demands (navigation experience). The correlation between years of taxi driving and posterior hippocampus size suggests the brain adapts structurally to spatial demands.
How does Maguire (2000) demonstrate localization of function?
Indicates the posterior hippocampus is involved in using previously learned spatial information (navigation in taxi drivers), while the anterior hippocampus may be more involved in encoding new spatial layouts.
What are the strengths of Maguire’s (2000) study?
Single-blind analysis reduced researcher bias. MRI has high ecological validity as participants weren’t tasked during scans. The correlation with years of driving supports neuroplasticity over innate differences. Ethically sound (consent, no health risks).
What are the limitations of Maguire’s (2000) study?
Quasi-experiment, so no cause-and-effect can be established. Sampling bias (all male participants) limits generalizability. Cannot fully rule out that those with larger hippocampi are drawn to taxi driving, though correlation with experience counters this.

Draganski (2004)

What is the background concept of neuroplasticity in Draganski (2004)?
Neuroplasticity is the brain’s ability to change its neural structures in response to the environment and cognition. It involves dendritic branching (via Long Term Potentiation, LTP) from repeated tasks, increasing neural density, and neural pruning when behaviors stop, reducing unused synaptic connections.
What was the aim of Draganski’s (2004) study?
To investigate whether learning a new skill (juggling) would affect the brain’s structure, specifically looking for changes in grey matter density as evidence of neuroplasticity.
Who were the participants in Draganski’s (2004) study?
24 volunteers aged 20–24 (21 females, 3 males), all non-jugglers at the start. They were divided into two groups: jugglers (taught to juggle) and non-jugglers (control group).
What was the procedure of Draganski’s (2004) study?
All participants had an initial MRI scan. The juggling group was taught a three-ball cascade routine, practiced until mastered, then had a second MRI scan. They stopped juggling for three months and had a third MRI scan. The non-juggling group had MRIs at the same intervals. Voxel-based morphometry (VBM) analyzed grey matter density.
What were the key findings of Draganski’s (2004) study?
After mastering juggling, the juggling group showed increased grey matter in the mid-temporal area (visual memory) of both hemispheres. Three months after stopping, grey matter decreased in these areas. The non-juggling group showed no changes throughout the study.
How does Draganski (2004) demonstrate neuroplasticity?
Shows that learning a skill (juggling) increases grey matter in the mid-temporal area (via LTP and dendritic branching), and stopping the skill leads to neural pruning, reducing grey matter, demonstrating the brain’s structural adaptability.
How does Draganski (2004) relate to the formation of synapses and neural pruning?
Juggling increased neural density via synapse formation (LTP and dendritic branching) in the mid-temporal area. Stopping juggling led to neural pruning, reducing grey matter as unused synaptic connections were eliminated.
What research methods does Draganski (2004) use to study the brain?
Experimental pre-test, post-test design with a control group. Used MRI scans and voxel-based morphometry (VBM) to measure changes in grey matter density over time, showing structural brain changes due to behavior.
What are the strengths of Draganski’s (2004) study?
Experimental design with a control group supports cause-and-effect (juggling caused brain changes). Pre-test, post-test design tracks changes over time. VBM provides precise measurement of grey matter changes.
What are the limitations of Draganski’s (2004) study?
Small sample size (24 participants) may reduce reliability of averages. Internal validity issues as participants practiced at home. Needs replication to confirm reliability. Gender imbalance (mostly female) may limit generalizability.

Loftus & Palmer (1974)

What concept did Loftus & Palmer (1974) investigate, and how does it relate to Bartlett (1932)?
Investigated reconstructive memory—how schemas influence memory. Bartlett (1932) showed cultural schemas distort memory by fitting experiences into existing knowledge. Loftus & Palmer tested how leading questions (post-event information) distort memory, like speed estimates in car accidents.
What was the aim of Experiment 1 in Loftus & Palmer (1974)?
To investigate whether leading questions (using different verbs) affect the estimation of speed in a car accident viewed on film, testing the influence of wording on memory.
What was the procedure of Experiment 1 in Loftus & Palmer (1974)?
45 students were divided into 5 groups (independent samples design). They watched 7 films of car accidents (5–30 seconds long). After each film, they gave an account of the accident and answered a questionnaire. The critical question varied: “About how fast were the cars going when they [smashed/collided/bumped/hit/contacted] each other?” Speed estimates were recorded.
What were the results of Experiment 1 in Loftus & Palmer (1974)?
Mean speed estimates varied by verb: “smashed” (40.8 mph), “collided” (39.3 mph), “bumped” (38.1 mph), “hit” (34.0 mph), “contacted” (31.8 mph). Results were significant (p ≤ 0.005), showing the verb influenced speed estimates, likely due to schema activation or response bias.
What was the aim of Experiment 2 in Loftus & Palmer (1974)?
To investigate if leading questions causing higher speed estimates (e.g., “smashed”) would lead participants to falsely recall broken glass in the accident, testing schema processing in reconstructive memory.
What was the procedure of Experiment 2 in Loftus & Palmer (1974)?
150 students were randomly assigned to 3 groups. They watched a 1-minute film of a car accident (4 seconds of crash). Group 1 was asked, “How fast were the cars going when they smashed into each other?” Group 2 was asked the same with “hit.” Group 3 wasn’t asked about speed. One week later, all answered a questionnaire with the critical question: “Did you see any broken glass?” (There was none in the film.)
What were the results of Experiment 2 in Loftus & Palmer (1974)?
“Smashed” group estimated higher speeds (10.46 mph) than “hit” group (8.00 mph). More in the “smashed” group (16) reported seeing broken glass compared to “hit” (7) and control (6). Chi-square test was significant (p ≤ 0.025), showing “smashed” led to more false memories of broken glass due to schema activation.
How does Loftus & Palmer (1974) demonstrate the reliability of cognitive processes?
Shows memory (reconstructive memory) is unreliable. Leading questions (e.g., “smashed” vs. “hit”) distort recall, as schemas activated by wording change memory (e.g., higher speed estimates, false recall of broken glass), highlighting memory’s susceptibility to post-event information.
What research methods does Loftus & Palmer (1974) use in the cognitive approach?
Lab experiments with controlled variables (IV: verb in question; DV: speed estimate, recall of broken glass). Independent samples design (Experiment 1) and random allocation (Experiment 2). Used statistical tests (p ≤ 0.005, p ≤ 0.025) to confirm significance, establishing cause-and-effect between leading questions and memory distortion.
What are the strengths of Loftus & Palmer’s (1974) study?
Controlled lab experiment minimized confounding variables, establishing cause-and-effect (leading questions distort memory). Statistical significance (p ≤ 0.005, p ≤ 0.025) supports reliability of findings. Practical application to eyewitness testimony in court settings.
What are the limitations of Loftus & Palmer’s (1974) study?
Low ecological validity—watching films lacks the emotional impact of real accidents, limiting generalizability. Student sample (young, inexperienced drivers) may not represent the general population. Artificial setting (lab) may not reflect real-life memory processes.
